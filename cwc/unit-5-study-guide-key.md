# **Unit 5 Study Guide - Answer Key**

## **Dividing the Kingdom (Lesson 5.1)**

**What mistake did Rehoboam make that led to the kingdom splitting?**

Rehoboam rejected the wise counsel of the elders and followed the harsh advice of his younger advisors. Instead of lightening the people's burden, he threatened to make it heavier, which caused the northern tribes to rebel.

**Why did Jeroboam set up golden calves at Bethel and Dan—what fear drove this decision?**

Jeroboam feared that if the people kept going to Jerusalem to worship, they would give their loyalty back to Rehoboam and the house of David. To keep political control, he set up rival worship centers with golden calves.

**What were the tragic results of Jeroboam's poor decision?**

Jeroboam's calves led Israel into ongoing idolatry and false worship. His sin became the pattern for the northern kingdom and helped lead to Israel's eventual destruction.

## **Early Kings of the Northern Kingdom (Lesson 5.2)**

**What pattern does the author of 1 Kings use to introduce and evaluate each king of Israel?**

The author gives a standard pattern: when the king began to reign, how long he reigned, and whether he did evil or good in the eyes of the Lord. Israel's kings are repeatedly judged by whether they followed Jeroboam's sinful pattern.

**What does the Bible say about Omri, and how does that differ from the way the world might measure his success?**

The Bible says Omri did more evil than the kings before him. From a worldly point of view, he looked successful because he built Samaria, strengthened the kingdom, and became well known internationally. The contrast shows that God judges by faithfulness, not outward success.

**What do these early northern kings teach us about what God considers truly important in a person's life?**

They show that obedience to God matters more than power, fame, wealth, or political success. A person can look successful to the world and still be a failure in God's eyes.

## **God vs. Baal (Lesson 5.3)**

**How did Ahab's actions surpass the evil of previous kings?**

Ahab married Jezebel, built a temple for Baal in Samaria, set up an Asherah pole, and promoted Baal worship openly. Scripture says he did more to provoke the Lord than the kings before him.

**What role did Jezebel play in establishing Baal worship in Israel? Why was Baal worship so attractive and so dangerous to the Israelites?**

Jezebel aggressively promoted Baal worship and supported its prophets. Baal worship was attractive because Baal was seen as the god of rain, fertility, and crops, so people thought he could provide what they needed. It was dangerous because it drew Israel away from exclusive devotion to the Lord and led them into idolatry and pagan practices.

**Describe the showdown between Elijah and the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel. What did this demonstrate about God's power?**

Elijah challenged 450 prophets of Baal to prepare a sacrifice and call on their god for fire. They cried out all day, but Baal gave no answer. Elijah repaired the Lord's altar, drenched the sacrifice with water, prayed, and God sent fire from heaven that consumed the sacrifice, the wood, the stones, and the water. This proved that the Lord alone is the true God and that Baal had no power.

## **Ahab's Legacy (Lesson 5.4)**

**How did Ahab and Jezebel abandon God's covenant in the story of Naboth's vineyard?**

Ahab wanted Naboth's vineyard, but Naboth refused because it was his family inheritance. Jezebel arranged false accusations so Naboth would be executed, and Ahab took the vineyard. They abused royal power, rejected God's law, and destroyed a covenant member for personal gain.

**What sentence did God pronounce on Ahab through Elijah, and how was it fulfilled?**

God declared that dogs would lick up Ahab's blood where they had licked Naboth's blood, and that Ahab's house would be judged. Later Ahab was killed in battle, his blood was washed from the chariot, and dogs licked it up just as Elijah had prophesied.

**What does Ahab's life show about political success, personal greed, and the certainty of God's prophetic word?**

Ahab shows that outward success cannot hide a wicked heart. His greed and compromise led to injustice and judgment, and every word God spoke against him through the prophets came true.

## **God's Judgment on Israel (Lesson 5.5)**

**What positive changes did Jehu bring to Israel? What critical failure prevented Israel from fully returning to God?**

Jehu destroyed the house of Ahab, wiped out Baal worship, and tore down Baal's temple. But he refused to remove Jeroboam's golden calves at Bethel and Dan, so his reform was only partial.

**Why did God allow the Assyrians to destroy Israel, according to 2 Kings 17?**

God allowed Israel to be destroyed because they worshiped other gods, followed pagan practices, rejected God's covenant, and refused to listen to the prophets he kept sending. After long patience and many warnings, God removed them from his presence.

**Which kingdom fell first: Israel (the Northern Kingdom) or Judah (the Southern Kingdom)?**

Israel, the Northern Kingdom, fell first.

**Even in judgment, how did God show that he was not finished with his covenant people?**

God kept sending prophets to warn his people, preserved Judah and David's line, and brought a remnant of Israelites back to worship him. His covenant purposes did not end even in judgment.

## **Judah: The First 150 Years (Lesson 5.6)**

**How does Chronicles judge each king?**

Chronicles judges each king primarily by his relationship to God: whether he sought the Lord, obeyed God's law, upheld true worship, and honored God's covenant with David.

**What reforms did Asa carry out early in his reign, and what broke down at the end of his life?**

Early in his reign, Asa removed idols and foreign altars, commanded Judah to seek the Lord, and led covenant renewal. Later he stopped depending on God, relied on political alliances, reacted badly to prophetic correction, and even in sickness did not seek the Lord as he should have.

**Why does Chronicles keep emphasizing that God preserved David's line even through unfaithful kings?**

Chronicles emphasizes this because God had made a covenant with David that his line would continue. Even when kings failed, God preserved the dynasty because his promise pointed forward to the coming Messiah, the true Son of David.

## **A Case Study of a Godly King: Hezekiah (Lesson 5.7)**

**What religious reforms did Hezekiah implement in Judah?**

Hezekiah reopened and cleansed the temple, consecrated the Levites, restored sacrifices, renewed covenant worship, and led the people in celebrating Passover.

**How did Hezekiah demonstrate reliance on God during his fatal illness? What was God's response, and how did he confirm it?**

Hezekiah prayed, wept, and cried out to God when Isaiah told him he would die. God answered by granting him fifteen more years of life, and he confirmed the promise by causing the shadow to move backward.

**Why did the Babylonians visit Hezekiah, and what did he foolishly show them? What was Isaiah's prophetic response?**

The Babylonians visited after hearing about Hezekiah's illness and recovery, likely also with political interest. Hezekiah foolishly showed them all his treasures, storehouses, and military resources. Isaiah responded by prophesying that Babylon would one day carry away all those treasures and that some of Hezekiah's descendants would be taken into exile.

## **Judah's Decline (Lesson 5.8)**

**What extreme idolatry did Manasseh introduce to Judah?**

Manasseh rebuilt high places, set up Baal and Asherah worship, placed idols in the temple, practiced sorcery and divination, and even sacrificed his sons. He led Judah into worse evil than the nations God had driven out.

**What significant event occurred during Josiah's temple repairs?**

During the repairs, the Book of the Law was rediscovered in the temple and read to Josiah.

**Why couldn't even Josiah's good reforms prevent Judah's judgment? What does this teach about external reforms vs. internal heart transformation?**

Josiah's reforms were real and good, but they could not undo generations of sin or change the people's hearts permanently. This shows that outward reform alone is not enough; people need inward heart transformation.

## **The Fall of Judah (Lesson 5.9)**

**Describe the Babylonian siege of Jerusalem. How long did it last? What were conditions like inside the city?**

The Babylonians besieged Jerusalem for about eighteen months. Conditions inside became desperate, with severe famine and suffering before the city walls were finally broken through.

**What happened to the temple and the city of Jerusalem? What happened to the people who remained in the land?**

The Babylonians burned the temple, the palace, and the important buildings of Jerusalem, and they tore down the city walls. Most of the people were killed or taken into exile, while only the poorest were left in the land.

**What hope is given at the end of 2 Kings with Jehoiachin's release?**

Jehoiachin's release shows that the line of David was still alive and that exile was not the end of God's plan. It leaves a note of hope that restoration is still possible.

**What promises did the prophets give to carry the exiles through captivity?**

The prophets promised that after the appointed time God would bring the exiles back, comfort his people, restore them, and give them hope for the future. They promised that God's covenant love had not failed.
