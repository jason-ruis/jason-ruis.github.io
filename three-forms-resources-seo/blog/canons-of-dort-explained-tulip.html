Canons of Dort Explained: Understanding TULIP and the Five Points of Calvinism

Published January 18, 2025 | 12 min read

The Canons of Dort and the Five Points of Calvinism (TULIP) represent the Reformed Church's understanding of God's sovereignty in salvation. This comprehensive guide explains each point with biblical support, historical context, and practical implications for Christian living.

What Are the Canons of Dort?

The Canons of Dort are Reformed confessional statements adopted at the international Synod of Dort (1618-1619). They were written in response to the theological errors of Arminianism, which challenged traditional Reformed teaching on salvation, grace, and predestination.

The Synod brought together 84 Dutch delegates and 26 international representatives from England, Scotland, Germany, and Switzerland. After seven months of careful deliberation (154 sessions), the assembly unanimously affirmed what became known as the Five Points of Calvinism.

Why "TULIP"?

The acronym TULIP is a helpful memory device for the Five Points, though it came into use later than the Canons themselves. Each letter represents a core doctrine:

  • T - Total Depravity
  • U - Unconditional Election
  • L - Limited Atonement
  • I - Irresistible Grace
  • P - Perseverance of the Saints

Let's explore each point in depth.

T - Total Depravity

What It Means

Total Depravity teaches that sin has affected every part of human nature—mind, will, emotions, and body. This doesn't mean people are as bad as they could possibly be, but that sin touches everything about us, leaving us spiritually dead and unable to save ourselves or even choose God apart from His grace.

The Canons State:

"Man after the fall begot children in his own likeness. A corrupt stock produced a corrupt offspring...Therefore all men are conceived in sin, and are by nature children of wrath, incapable of any saving good, prone to evil, dead in sin, and in bondage thereto" (Third/Fourth Head, Article 2-3).

Biblical Support

  • Romans 3:10-12 - "None is righteous, no, not one; no one understands; no one seeks for God"
  • Romans 8:7-8 - "The mind that is set on the flesh is hostile to God, for it does not submit to God's law; indeed, it cannot. Those who are in the flesh cannot please God"
  • Ephesians 2:1-3 - "You were dead in the trespasses and sins...carrying out the desires of the body and the mind, and were by nature children of wrath"
  • John 6:44 - "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him"

What It Doesn't Mean

Total Depravity does not mean humans are incapable of any good works or kindness. People can still show love, create beauty, and act morally. However, even our best works are tainted by sin and cannot earn salvation. We are spiritually unable to save ourselves or even desire God without His intervening grace.

U - Unconditional Election

What It Means

Unconditional Election teaches that before the foundation of the world, God chose certain people for salvation based solely on His sovereign will, not on anything foreseen in them. Election is not based on our faith, works, or merit—it is entirely God's gracious choice.

The Canons State:

"Election is the unchangeable purpose of God, whereby, before the foundation of the world, he has out of mere grace...chosen from the whole human race...a certain number of persons to redemption in Christ, whom he...gave to Christ" (First Head, Article 7).

Biblical Support

  • Ephesians 1:4-5 - "He chose us in him before the foundation of the world...having predestined us for adoption to himself as sons through Jesus Christ, according to the purpose of his will"
  • Romans 9:11-16 - "Though they were not yet born...in order that God's purpose of election might continue...it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy"
  • John 15:16 - "You did not choose me, but I chose you"
  • Acts 13:48 - "As many as were appointed to eternal life believed"

Common Questions

Q: Doesn't this make God unfair?
A: No one deserves salvation; we all deserve judgment because of sin. Election is an act of pure grace. God would be perfectly just to leave all humanity in sin. The marvel is that He chose to save anyone.

Q: Does this mean we shouldn't evangelize?
A: No! God uses means to accomplish His purposes. He ordained not only the end (who will be saved) but also the means (the preaching of the gospel). We evangelize in obedience to Christ's command, trusting God will save His elect through the proclamation of the Word.

L - Limited Atonement (Particular Redemption)

What It Means

Limited Atonement (better called Particular Redemption or Definite Atonement) teaches that Christ died specifically to save the elect. His death actually accomplished redemption for those whom the Father gave Him, not merely made salvation possible for everyone.

The Canons State:

"It was the will of God that Christ...should effectually redeem out of every people, tribe, nation, and language, all those, and those only, who were from eternity chosen to salvation and given to him by the Father" (Second Head, Article 8).

Biblical Support

  • John 10:11, 15 - "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep...I lay down my life for the sheep"
  • Matthew 1:21 - "He will save his people from their sins" (not make salvation possible)
  • Ephesians 5:25 - "Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her"
  • John 17:9 - "I am praying for them. I am not praying for the world but for those whom you have given me"
  • Hebrews 9:12 - "He secured an eternal redemption" (not made it possible)

Clarifications

Limited Atonement doesn't mean Christ's death was insufficient to save everyone if God had chosen to do so. Rather, it teaches that Christ's death was intended to actually save the elect and that it accomplished what God intended—certain salvation for His people.

Some prefer the term "Definite Atonement" because it emphasizes that Christ's death definitely saved those for whom it was intended, rather than merely making salvation possible for all.

I - Irresistible Grace (Effectual Calling)

What It Means

Irresistible Grace (also called Effectual Calling) teaches that when God calls His elect to salvation, His grace is so powerful that it overcomes all resistance and brings them to faith. The Holy Spirit's work in regeneration cannot be ultimately resisted by those whom God has chosen.

The Canons State:

God "powerfully illuminates their minds by his Holy Spirit...He penetrates into the inmost recesses of man; he opens the closed and softens the hardened heart...infuses new qualities into the will...so that they, who were dead, are made alive" (Third/Fourth Head, Articles 11-12).

Biblical Support

  • John 6:37 - "All that the Father gives me will come to me" (not merely might come)
  • John 6:44 - "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him"
  • Romans 8:30 - "Those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified" (100% success rate)
  • Philippians 2:13 - "It is God who works in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure"
  • Ezekiel 36:26 - "I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh"

Important Distinctions

"Irresistible" doesn't mean people never resist God's grace in one sense. The unregenerate constantly resist the general call of the gospel. However, when God regenerates the heart, giving spiritual life, the person willingly comes to Christ. The grace is irresistible in that it accomplishes its purpose—God always succeeds in saving those He intends to save.

P - Perseverance of the Saints

What It Means

Perseverance of the Saints (also called Eternal Security) teaches that those whom God has called and justified will certainly persevere in faith to the end and can never lose their salvation. God preserves His people, and they will persevere.

The Canons State:

"By reason of these remains of indwelling sin, and the temptations of sin and of the world...they cannot always maintain this full assurance of faith. But God, who is rich in mercy...confirms and powerfully preserves them therein, even to the end" (Fifth Head, Articles 3, 8).

Biblical Support

  • Philippians 1:6 - "He who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ"
  • John 10:27-29 - "I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand...and no one is able to snatch them out of the Father's hand"
  • Romans 8:38-39 - "Nothing...will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord"
  • 1 Peter 1:5 - "By God's power [you] are being guarded through faith for a salvation ready to be revealed"
  • Jude 24 - "Now to him who is able to keep you from stumbling"

Balanced Understanding

Perseverance doesn't mean true believers never sin or never doubt. Christians struggle with sin and sometimes fall into serious sin. However, God will not allow His elect to finally fall away. He disciplines His children and brings them to repentance.

This doctrine also doesn't give license to sin. Rather, it provides assurance that our salvation depends on God's faithfulness, not our own. We persevere because God preserves us.

How the Five Points Work Together

The Five Points are not isolated doctrines but form a unified system of theology:

  1. Because of Total Depravity, we cannot save ourselves
  2. So God chose us through Unconditional Election
  3. Christ died to actually save the elect through Limited Atonement
  4. The Holy Spirit effectually calls the elect through Irresistible Grace
  5. God ensures they persevere through Perseverance of the Saints

Together, these doctrines present salvation as entirely the work of the Triune God—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—from beginning to end.

Why These Doctrines Matter

For Worship

Understanding God's sovereignty in salvation leads to deeper worship. If salvation is entirely God's work, He alone deserves all glory. This truth humbles us and magnifies God's grace.

For Assurance

These doctrines provide tremendous comfort. Our salvation doesn't depend on our fluctuating faith or imperfect obedience but on God's unchangeable purpose and power.

For Evangelism

Knowing that God saves His elect through the preaching of the gospel motivates us to evangelize boldly. We don't know who the elect are, so we proclaim the gospel to all, trusting God to save whom He will.

For Christian Living

These truths encourage holy living. God's people, saved by grace, respond with gratitude and obedience. We don't work to earn salvation but because we've been saved.

Common Objections Answered

"This makes God the author of sin"

No. The Canons explicitly reject this (First Head, rejection 1). God ordains all things, but He is not the author of sin. He accomplishes His purposes through secondary causes while humans remain responsible for their choices.

"This undermines human responsibility"

Scripture affirms both divine sovereignty and human responsibility. We are commanded to believe (Acts 16:31), and we are held accountable for our response to the gospel. Yet God must give us new hearts to believe (Ezekiel 36:26).

"This makes evangelism pointless"

The opposite! God has ordained not only the end (who will be saved) but the means (preaching the gospel). The Reformed tradition has produced some of history's greatest evangelists and missionaries precisely because they trusted God's sovereign grace.

Conclusion

The Canons of Dort and the Five Points of Calvinism present a God-centered view of salvation that gives all glory to God. From election before the foundation of the world to preservation unto eternal life, God is the Author and Perfecter of our faith.

These doctrines are not dry theology but living truth that shapes how we worship, pray, evangelize, and live. They remind us that "salvation belongs to the Lord" (Jonah 2:9) and that He will accomplish all His purposes.
