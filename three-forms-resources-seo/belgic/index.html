What is the Belgic Confession?

The Belgic Confession (also known as the Confessio Belgica) is one of the oldest and most important Reformed confessional documents. Written in 1561 by Guido de Brès, a Reformed pastor in the Netherlands, it was composed during a time of severe persecution of Protestants under Spanish Catholic rule.

De Brès wrote this confession to demonstrate to the authorities that Reformed believers were not revolutionaries but faithful Christians who held to biblical doctrine. Tragically, he was martyred for his faith in 1567, just six years after writing this confession. His work, however, lived on and became a foundational document for Reformed churches worldwide.

Structure of the Belgic Confession

The Belgic Confession consists of 37 articles organized around major Christian doctrines:

  • Articles 1-11 - God, Scripture, and the Trinity
  • Articles 12-15 - Creation and the Fall
  • Articles 16-26 - Salvation through Christ
  • Articles 27-36 - The Church and Sacraments
  • Article 37 - The Last Judgment

Why Study the Belgic Confession?

The Belgic Confession is valued for its:

  • Biblical foundation - Each article is grounded in Scripture with extensive proof texts
  • Clear doctrine - Articulates Reformed theology systematically and comprehensively
  • Historical significance - Written in the context of persecution, showing the cost of faithful confession
  • Ecumenical value - Recognized by Reformed churches globally
  • Pastoral application - Addresses practical matters of church life and Christian living

Historical Context

In 1561, Reformed believers in the Low Countries (modern-day Netherlands and Belgium) faced brutal persecution from Catholic authorities. Thousands were martyred for their Protestant faith. Guido de Brès wrote the Belgic Confession to:

  1. Show that Reformed believers held orthodox Christian doctrine
  2. Distinguish Reformed faith from Anabaptist radicalism
  3. Provide a clear statement of belief for Reformed congregations
  4. Appeal to the conscience of persecuting authorities

The confession was presented to King Philip II of Spain in 1562, but persecution continued. De Brès himself was hanged in 1567. Despite this, the Belgic Confession was officially adopted by the Synod of Dort in 1619 and remains a standard of faith for millions of Reformed Christians today.

Key Articles of the Belgic Confession

Article 1: The Only God

"We all believe in our hearts and confess with our mouths that there is a single and simple spiritual being, whom we call God—eternal, incomprehensible, invisible, unchangeable, infinite, almighty; completely wise, just, and good, and the overflowing source of all good."

Article 2: How God Makes Himself Known to Us

"We know him by two means: First, by the creation, preservation, and government of the universe...Second, he makes himself known to us more clearly by his holy and divine Word..."

Article 22: The Righteousness of Faith

"We believe that, for us to acquire the true knowledge of this great mystery, the Holy Spirit kindles in our hearts a true faith that embraces Jesus Christ...and appropriates him and all his merits to itself."

Article 27: The Holy Catholic Church

"We believe and confess one single catholic or universal church—a holy congregation and gathering of true Christian believers, awaiting their entire salvation in Jesus Christ, being washed by his blood, and sanctified and sealed by the Holy Spirit."

Frequently Asked Questions

How many articles are in the Belgic Confession?

The Belgic Confession contains 37 articles covering all major areas of Christian doctrine.

Who wrote the Belgic Confession?

The Belgic Confession was written by Guido de Brès, a Reformed pastor in the Netherlands, in 1561. He was martyred for his faith in 1567.

Where can I read the Belgic Confession for free?

The Belgic Confession is public domain and available free from CRCNA.org and other Reformed denominational websites.

What is the best commentary on the Belgic Confession?

For most readers, Daniel Hyde's "With Heart and Mouth" is excellent. For advanced study, David Engelsma's 2-volume commentary or Nicolaas Gootjes' historical study are highly recommended.

Why is it called the "Belgic" Confession?

The name comes from the Latin Belgica, referring to the Low Countries (modern Netherlands and Belgium) where it was written. It's sometimes called the "Netherlands Confession."
