What is the Heidelberg Catechism?

The Heidelberg Catechism is one of the most beloved Reformed confessional documents, written in 1563 in Heidelberg, Germany. Commissioned by Elector Frederick III, it was primarily authored by Zacharias Ursinus and Caspar Olevianus.

The catechism consists of 129 questions and answers divided into 52 Lord's Days for weekly instruction throughout the year. It teaches the Christian faith through three main themes:

  • Misery (Questions 1-11) - Our sin and need for salvation
  • Deliverance (Questions 12-85) - Redemption through Christ
  • Gratitude (Questions 86-129) - Living the Christian life

The catechism begins with the famous Question 1: "What is your only comfort in life and in death?" The answer beautifully expresses assurance in Christ: "That I am not my own, but belong—body and soul, in life and in death—to my faithful Savior, Jesus Christ."

Why Study the Heidelberg Catechism?

The Heidelberg Catechism is valued for its:

  • Warm, personal tone - Uses "I" and "my" to emphasize personal faith
  • Biblical foundation - Each answer is rooted in Scripture
  • Pastoral emphasis - Focuses on comfort and assurance
  • Comprehensive doctrine - Covers all major Christian teachings
  • Practical application - Connects theology to daily life

Frequently Asked Questions

How many questions are in the Heidelberg Catechism?

The Heidelberg Catechism contains 129 questions and answers, divided into 52 Lord's Days for weekly study throughout the year.

What is Question 1 of the Heidelberg Catechism?

"What is your only comfort in life and in death?" Answer: "That I am not my own, but belong—body and soul, in life and in death—to my faithful Savior, Jesus Christ."

Where can I read the Heidelberg Catechism for free?

The Heidelberg Catechism is public domain and available free from many Reformed denominational websites including CRCNA.org and Heidelberg-Catechism.com.

What is the best commentary on the Heidelberg Catechism?

For beginners, Kevin DeYoung's "The Good News We Almost Forgot" is excellent. For advanced study, Zacharias Ursinus's original commentary or R. Scott Clark's new 2025 commentary are highly recommended.
