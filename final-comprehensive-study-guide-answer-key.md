# **Old Testament Comprehensive Study Guide - Answer Key**

# **Unit 1: Genesis**

## **Overall Unit Themes**

### What is the overall theme of the Old Testament?
**Answer:** God is a promise-maker and promise-keeper who works through history to bring salvation to His people, ultimately pointing to redemption through Jesus Christ — God is faithful. His people are not. *(From Lesson 1.1)*

### What is a covenant?
**Answer:** A covenant is a binding agreement between two parties that establishes a relationship with specific obligations. — A promise. *(From Lesson 1.1)*

### Why is God's faithfulness to His promises a central theme?
**Answer:** God's faithfulness is central because He always keeps His promises even when humans fail, demonstrating His unchanging character and love. *(From Lesson 1.1)*

## **Creation to Babel—Genesis 1-11 (Lessons 1.2-5)**

### What special role and responsibility did God give to humans as bearers of the Imago Dei?
**Answer:** Humans were made in God's image (Imago Dei) and given dominion over creation, serving as God's representatives to rule, care for, and steward the earth on His behalf. *(From Lesson 1.2)*

### What choice did the tree of the knowledge of good and evil represent for Adam and Eve?
**Answer:** The tree represented the choice between trusting and obeying God or choosing autonomy—deciding for themselves what was good and evil instead of accepting God's definition. *(From Lesson 1.3)*

### Describe the Noahic Covenant. Was it conditional or unconditional? What was its sign?
**Answer:** The Noahic Covenant was God's promise never to destroy the earth by flood again; it was unconditional (requiring nothing from Noah or humanity), and its sign was the rainbow. *(From Lesson 1.4)*

---

## **The Patriarchs—Genesis 12-33 (Lessons 1.6-10)**

### List the three main promises God made to Abram in the Abrahamic Covenant.
**Answer:** (1) Land—God would give Abram and his descendants a specific land; (2) Descendants—God would make Abram into a great nation; (3) Blessing—Through Abram, all nations of the earth would be blessed. *(From Lesson 1.6)*

### Explain how the covenant ceremony in Genesis 15 showed that the covenant was unconditional.
**Answer:** In the ceremony, God alone passed between the divided animals while Abram slept, indicating that God took sole responsibility for keeping the covenant—if the covenant failed, God Himself would bear the consequences, not Abram. *(From Lesson 1.7)*

### Describe the event that transformed Jacob's identity. What was his name changed to, and what does it signify?
**Answer:** Jacob wrestled with a mysterious figure (the Angel of the Lord) at the Jabbok River and was renamed "Israel," meaning "he struggles with God" or "God struggles"—signifying that Jacob's descendants would carry this identity as God's covenant people. *(From Lesson 1.10)*

## **The Joseph Narrative—Genesis 37-50 (Lessons 1.11-14)**

### Why did Joseph test his brothers?
**Answer:** Joseph tested his brothers to see if they had truly changed—whether they would abandon Benjamin (the new favored son) the way they had abandoned him. *(From Lesson 1.12)*

### What did Joseph need to see in his brothers?
**Answer:** Joseph needed to see if they would protect Benjamin at their own expense rather than abandoning him like they had Joseph, demonstrating genuine repentance and change. *(From Lesson 1.12)*

### How does Joseph's forgiveness of his brothers point them toward God's larger purpose?
**Answer:** Joseph's forgiveness demonstrated that what his brothers meant for evil, God meant for good—to save many lives, pointing them to trust in God's sovereign plan that works through even sinful human actions. *(From Lesson 1.14)*

### What is the meaning and importance of Genesis 50:20?
**Answer:** "You intended to harm me, but God intended it for good to accomplish what is now being done, the saving of many lives"—this verse summarizes the theme that God sovereignly works through human sin and suffering to accomplish His redemptive purposes. *(From Lesson 1.14)*

### How does Genesis 50:20 summarize the entire story and one of the main themes of the unit?
**Answer:** The verse ties together Joseph's story by showing that God used the brothers' evil actions to position Joseph in Egypt to save many lives during the famine—foreshadowing how God's plan of salvation works through the brokenness of human history. *(From Lesson 1.14)*

# **Unit 2: Exodus through Deuteronomy**

## **God's Preparation, Calling, Power, & Deliverance (Lessons 2.1-4)**

### What does the name "I AM WHO I AM" reveal about God's character and His relationship with His people?
**Answer:** "I AM WHO I AM" (YHWH) reveals that God is self-existent, eternal, unchanging, and faithful—He is not defined by anything outside Himself and will always be who He promises to be in relationship with His people. *(From Lesson 2.2)*

### How do the plagues demonstrate God's supremacy over Egyptian gods?
**Answer:** Each plague specifically targeted and defeated an Egyptian deity (e.g., the Nile god, sun god Ra), demonstrating that the God of Israel was the one true God with power over all creation and all false gods. *(From Lesson 2.3)*

## **God's Provision, Law, Dwelling, & Holiness (Lessons 2.5-8)**

### Describe the complaint cycle that repeated in Israel's wilderness experience.
**Answer:** The pattern was: Israel faced a problem, they complained against Moses and God (often wanting to return to Egypt), God provided for their needs, and yet they soon forgot His faithfulness and complained again when the next challenge arose. *(From Lesson 2.5)*

### What does it mean that God's law required obedience in "both thought and deed"?
**Answer:** Unlike other ancient law codes that only governed actions, the Ten Commandments addressed internal attitudes (like coveting) as well as external behavior, showing that God cares about the heart, not just outward compliance. *(From Lesson 2.6)*

### Where was the tabernacle placed in the Israelite camp?
**Answer:** God instructed that the tabernacle be placed at the center of the camp, surrounded by the twelve tribes. *(From Lesson 2.7)*

### What was the significance of the tabernacle's location?
**Answer:** The central location signified that God was to be at the center of their lives, not just their geography. *(From Lesson 2.7)*

### What does it mean for God's people to be holy?
**Answer:** "Holy" means "set apart" for God's special purposes—His people must live differently from other nations in their worship and daily conduct. *(From Lesson 2.8)*

### Why is holiness the central theme of Leviticus?
**Answer:** Holiness is the central theme because God commanded, "Be holy because I am holy," establishing that His people's identity is defined by their relationship with and imitation of their holy God. *(From Lesson 2.8)*

---

## **Rebellion and Covenant Renewal (Lessons 2.9-10)**

### Compare the reports of the 10 spies versus Caleb and Joshua. What did they see differently?
**Answer:** All 12 spies confirmed the land was bountiful, but the 10 focused on the giants and fortified cities (obstacles), while Caleb and Joshua focused on God's power and promises, trusting He could give them the land. *(From Lesson 2.9)*

### What was God's judgment for Israel's unbelief at the border of the Promised Land?
**Answer:** God condemned the adult generation (except Caleb and Joshua) to wander in the wilderness for 40 years until they died—only those under 20 would enter the Promised Land. *(From Lesson 2.9)*

### Why did the new generation need to renew their covenant with God before entering the Promised Land?
**Answer:** This generation hadn't personally experienced Mount Sinai or the exodus—they needed to make their own covenant commitment to God. *(From Lesson 2.10)*

# **Unit 3: The Histories - Conquest and Early Monarchy**

## **Conquering, Settling, and Judges (Lessons 3.1-4)**

### What condition did Joshua give for the land to be a place of rest?
**Answer:** The land would be a place of rest for Israel only if they kept God's commands, remained loyal to Him alone, and did not intermarry with or worship the gods of the surrounding nations. *(From Lesson 3.2)*

### How was God's presence evident in Israel's victories and defeats?
**Answer:** Israel's victories and defeats depended entirely on God's presence with them—victory came when God was with them (Jericho), and defeat came when sin caused Him to withdraw His presence (Ai). *(From Lesson 3.1)*

### Describe the five-part cycle of life during the judges. What does each part mean?
**Answer:** (1) **Relapse**—Israel turned away from God into sin/idolatry; (2) **Retribution**—God allowed enemies to oppress them; (3) **Repentance**—in misery, they cried out to God; (4) **Rescue**—God raised up a judge to deliver them; (5) **Rest**—peace followed until the cycle began again. *(From Lesson 3.3)*

### What kind of heroes did God raise up? Why is this significant?
**Answer:** God raised up flawed heroes (like Gideon who was hesitant and Samson who was morally compromised) to show that deliverance came from God's power, not human perfection—pointing to humanity's need for a perfect Savior. *(From Lessons 3.3-3.4)*

## **Loyalty and Transition (Lessons 3.5-6)**

### How does Ruth's story contrast with life in Israel during the time of the judges?
**Answer:** While Israel "did what was right in their own eyes" and abandoned God, Ruth (a foreigner) chose to follow God faithfully, showing that godly living was possible even in spiritually dark times. *(From Lesson 3.5)*

### How did God work through Ruth to establish an important family line?
**Answer:** God blessed Ruth's faithfulness by giving her a son named Obed, who became the grandfather of King David, placing Ruth in the direct lineage of Jesus Christ. *(From Lesson 3.5)*

### What three offices did Samuel serve in Israel? Describe each role.
**Answer:** (1) **Prophet**—he received and proclaimed God's word to Israel; (2) **Priest**—he offered sacrifices and interceded for the people; (3) **Judge**—he settled disputes and led Israel against enemies. *(From Lesson 3.6)*


## **Saul's, David's, and Solomon's Reigns (Lessons 3.7-10)**

### Why did God eventually reject Saul as king? What was his key sin?
**Answer:** Saul disobeyed God by offering a sacrifice himself (usurping the priest's role) rather than waiting for Samuel as commanded; his core sin was trusting his own judgment over God's command and fearing people more than God. *(From Lesson 3.7)*

### How did David and Saul respond differently to God throughout 1 Samuel?
**Answer:** Saul heard God's commands but made excuses and failed to truly obey, while David listened and obeyed with genuine repentance when he sinned; Saul trusted himself while David trusted God's timing and power. *(From Lesson 3.8)*

### What covenant did God establish with David? What was promised about his kingdom?
**Answer:** God promised David that his house, kingdom, and throne would endure forever (2 Samuel 7:16)—a promise impossible for any human dynasty but ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, whose kingdom has no end. *(From Lesson 3.9)*

### What sin did David commit with Bathsheba?
**Answer:** David committed adultery with Bathsheba and murdered her husband Uriah to cover it up. *(From Lesson 3.10)*

### Did God forgive David?
**Answer:** Yes, God forgave David when he repented (Psalm 51). *(From Lesson 3.10)*

### Did the consequences of David's sin disappear? Explain.
**Answer:** No, the consequences did not disappear—Nathan's prophecies were fulfilled with the death of their first child, family chaos (Amnon's crime, Absalom's rebellion), and public shame. *(From Lesson 3.10)*

### What did Solomon request from God? What else did God give him?
**Answer:** Solomon asked for a discerning heart/wisdom to govern God's people well; God gave him not only unprecedented wisdom but also riches, honor, and fame beyond what he requested. *(From Lesson 3.11)*


# **Units 4-5: Old Testament Writings + Divided Kingdom**

## **The Psalms & Proverbs (Lesson 4.1-2)**

### What do the Psalms teach us about prayer?
**Answer:** The Psalms teach that we can bring every emotion to God—praise, lament, thanksgiving, confession—with complete honesty; they model prayer that begins with raw complaint but moves toward trust, showing that genuine relationship with God includes both joy and struggle. *(From Lesson 4.1)*

### Who wrote most of the proverbs?
**Answer:** King Solomon wrote most of the proverbs (he composed 3,000 proverbs according to 1 Kings 4:32). *(From Lesson 4.2)*

### What does Proverbs 1:7 say is "the beginning of knowledge"?
**Answer:** "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge, but fools despise wisdom and instruction"—meaning true wisdom starts with reverence for and submission to God's authority. *(From Lesson 4.2)*

## **Song of Songs & Ecclesiastes (Lesson 4.3-4)**

### How does Song of Songs 8:6-7 describe the power and value of love?
**Answer:** Love is described as "strong as death," "unyielding as the grave," burning like a "blazing fire"; many waters cannot quench it and rivers cannot sweep it away; nothing on earth is worth as much as true love—all wealth would be scorned if offered for it. *(From Lesson 4.3)*

### How does Ecclesiastes contrast life "under the sun" (without God) with life "above the sun/under heaven" (with God)?
**Answer:** Life "under the sun" (without God) is meaningless—wisdom, pleasure, wealth, and work all end in death and are forgotten; life "above the sun/under heaven" (with God) has meaning through relationship with the Creator, enjoying His gifts, and fearing/obeying Him. *(From Lesson 4.4)*

### What experiments did the Teacher try in his search for meaning?
**Answer:** The Teacher pursued wisdom/knowledge, pleasure/laughter, great building projects, possessions/wealth, and work/toil. *(From Lesson 4.4)*

### What did the Teacher conclude from his experiments?
**Answer:** He concluded all were "meaningless" because death cancels everything out "under the sun." *(From Lesson 4.4)*

### What is humanity's "whole duty" according to Ecclesiastes 12:13-14?
**Answer:** "Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole duty of man. For God will bring every deed into judgment"—meaning is found not in earthly pursuits but in relationship with God and obedience to Him. *(From Lesson 4.4)*

## **Job (Lesson 4.5)**

### What was the friends' explanation for Job's suffering?
**Answer:** The friends believed suffering = punishment for sin (retribution theology), so Job must have sinned to deserve his suffering. *(From Lesson 4.5)*

### Why was the friends' theology flawed?
**Answer:** Their theology was flawed because it ignores that innocent people do suffer, assumes blessing is payment for good behavior, and cannot account for spiritual realities like Satan's test. *(From Lesson 4.5)*

### Instead of answering Job's "why" questions, what did God do in Job 38-39?
**Answer:** God asked Job a series of rhetorical questions about creation ("Where were you when I laid the earth's foundation?") to show the vast difference between human understanding and God's infinite wisdom and power. *(From Lesson 4.5)*

### How did Job respond after hearing from God (Job 42:1-6)?
**Answer:** Job realized God is beyond understanding, regretted his bitter words, said "My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you," and repented "in dust and ashes." *(From Lesson 4.5)*

## **Divided Kingdom, God vs. Baal, & Ahab's Legacy (Lesson 5.1-3)**

### Why did Jeroboam set up golden calves at Bethel and Dan?
**Answer:** Jeroboam feared that if people traveled to Jerusalem's temple to worship, they would return their allegiance to Rehoboam and kill him—so he created "convenient worship" sites to keep political control. *(From Lesson 5.1)*

### What fear drove Jeroboam's decision to set up the golden calves?
**Answer:** Jeroboam feared losing political control—if people went to Jerusalem's temple, they might return their allegiance to the house of David and kill him. *(From Lesson 5.1)*

### What were the tragic results of Jeroboam's golden calves?
**Answer:** The people worshiped these idols and soon forgot God entirely—this set in motion events that would never be reversed and eventually led to Israel's destruction. *(From Lesson 5.1)*

### Describe the showdown between Elijah and the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel.
**Answer:** Elijah stood alone against 450 prophets of Baal who worked themselves into a frenzy; Elijah prayed quietly to God, who answered by sending fire from heaven. *(From Lesson 5.2)*

### What did the Mount Carmel showdown demonstrate about God's power?
**Answer:** It demonstrated that only the LORD could control fire, rain, and fertility, not Baal—showing God's absolute supremacy over all false gods. *(From Lesson 5.2)*

### Why did Elijah flee to Horeb after his victory?
**Answer:** After Jezebel's death threat, Elijah was physically exhausted, spiritually depleted, and felt alone—he had seen God's great victory but still faced deadly opposition. *(From Lesson 5.2)*

### How did God minister to Elijah?
**Answer:** God sent an angel with food and water, then appeared not in wind/earthquake/fire but in a gentle whisper, assuring Elijah that 7,000 had not bowed to Baal. *(From Lesson 5.2)*

### Which kingdom falls first: Israel (Northern Kingdom) or Judah (Southern Kingdom)? Why?
**Answer:** Israel (the Northern Kingdom) fell first (722 BC to Assyria) because they never had a single king who followed God—from Jeroboam's golden calves onward, they completely abandoned God and became indistinguishable from surrounding nations. *(From Lesson 5.3)*

## **Judah—Decline and Fall (Lesson 5.4-7)**

### How does Chronicles judge each king?
**Answer:** Chronicles evaluates each king by three criteria: (1) whether they maintained temple worship, (2) whether they obeyed God's law, and (3) whether they honored the Davidic succession—their relationship with God, not political achievements, determined their legacy. *(From Lesson 5.4)*

### Why did the Babylonians visit Hezekiah?
**Answer:** The Babylonians came to congratulate Hezekiah on his recovery from illness (likely to form an alliance against Assyria). *(From Lesson 5.5)*

### What did Hezekiah foolishly show the Babylonians?
**Answer:** Hezekiah foolishly showed them all his wealth, treasures, and armory—everything in his storehouses. *(From Lesson 5.5)*

### What was Isaiah's prophetic response to Hezekiah's actions?
**Answer:** Isaiah prophesied that all Judah's wealth would be carried off to Babylon and Hezekiah's descendants would serve as eunuchs in the Babylonian palace. *(From Lesson 5.5)*

### What significant event occurred during Josiah's temple repairs?
**Answer:** During temple cleaning/repairs, they discovered the Book of the Law (likely Deuteronomy) that had been lost and forgotten; when read to King Josiah, he tore his robes in grief, realizing Judah's deep covenant unfaithfulness. *(From Lesson 5.6)*

### Why couldn't even Josiah's good reforms prevent Judah's judgment?
**Answer:** Despite Josiah's extensive reforms, the people's hearts remained unchanged—after his death, kings immediately returned to evil ways. *(From Lesson 5.6)*

### What does this teach about external reforms vs. internal heart transformation?
**Answer:** This shows that external reforms cannot produce internal heart transformation; human effort alone cannot achieve lasting righteousness, pointing to our need for Christ's transforming work through the New Covenant. *(From Lesson 5.6)*

### Describe the Babylonian siege of Jerusalem.
**Answer:** The siege lasted at least 18 months with severe famine inside the city (Lamentations 2 records some resorted to cannibalism); eventually the walls were broken through. *(From Lesson 5.7)*

### What were conditions like inside the city during the Babylonian siege?
**Answer:** Conditions were desperate with severe famine—Lamentations 2 records that some people resorted to cannibalism. *(From Lesson 5.7)*

### What happened to the temple and the city of Jerusalem?
**Answer:** The temple and all important buildings were burned, and the city walls were destroyed. *(From Lesson 5.7)*

### What happened to the people who remained in the land?
**Answer:** The people were killed or exiled to Babylon—only the poorest remained to work the land. *(From Lesson 5.7)*

### What hope is given at the end of 2 Kings with Jehoiachin's release?
**Answer:** After 37 years of imprisonment, Evil-Merodach released Jehoiachin and gave him a seat of honor—a sign that the Davidic line survived and exile wouldn't last forever. *(From Lesson 5.7)*

### How does Cyrus's decree in 2 Chronicles 36:22-23 provide hope for restoration?
**Answer:** Cyrus's decree fulfilled Jeremiah's prophecy: "The LORD moved the heart of Cyrus," who authorized the exiles to return to Jerusalem and rebuild God's temple, demonstrating God's covenant faithfulness. *(From Lesson 5.7)*


# **Unit 6: The Prophets**

## **Introduction to the Prophets (Lessons 6.1-6.2)**

### What common themes did the prophets share in their messages to Israel and Judah?
**Answer:** Common themes included: 1) Warning of judgment for breaking the covenant; 2) Call to repent from wickedness to God, and 3) Promise of hope for those who repent. *(From Lesson 6.1)*

### Why was the prophetic call important?
**Answer:** The call established that prophets were chosen by God, not self-appointed; it validated their message to skeptical audiences. *(From Lesson 6.2)*

### How did the prophetic call authenticate the prophet's message?
**Answer:** The call gave them authority to confront kings and people, making them accountable to God rather than to human opinion. *(From Lesson 6.2)*

## **Justice: A Central Message of the Prophets (Lessons 6.3-6.4)**

### What does Isaiah say is the kind of fasting that pleases God? What specific actions should replace empty rituals?
**Answer:** God desires fasting that loosens chains of injustice, sets the oppressed free, feeds the hungry, shelters the homeless, and clothes the naked—actions that care for others, not empty rituals. *(From Lesson 6.4)*

### According to Micah 6:8, what three things does God require from his people?
**Answer:** (1) Act justly, (2) Love mercy, (3) Walk humbly with your God. *(From Lesson 6.4)*

### What does "act justly" mean?
**Answer:** "Act justly" means actively pursuing justice and treating others fairly. *(From Lesson 6.4)*

### What does "love mercy" mean?
**Answer:** "Love mercy" means showing passionate, committed compassion to those in need, not grudging duty. *(From Lesson 6.4)*

### What does "walk humbly with your God" mean?
**Answer:** "Walk humbly with your God" means living in ongoing, daily, humble relationship with God—walking daily by faith. *(From Lesson 6.4)*

## **Prophet, Priest, and King (Lesson 6.5)**

### What were the distinct roles of prophet, priest, and king? What did each one do?
**Answer:** **Prophet**—spoke God's words to the people and kings as God's messenger; **Priest**—led worship, offered sacrifices, and served as intercessor between God and people; **King**—God's anointed earthly ruler who led in battle and represented people to other nations. *(From Lesson 6.5)*

### How does Jesus fulfill the role of prophet more perfectly than those in the Old Testament?
**Answer:** Jesus is the perfect Prophet who not only speaks God's word but IS the Word made flesh (John 1:14). *(From Lesson 6.5)*

### How does Jesus fulfill the role of priest more perfectly than those in the Old Testament?
**Answer:** Jesus is the perfect Priest who offered Himself once for all as the final sacrifice (Hebrews 7:27) and is our only mediator between us and God. *(From Lesson 6.5)*

### How does Jesus fulfill the role of king more perfectly than those in the Old Testament?
**Answer:** Jesus is the perfect King who rules with justice and mercy eternally, never abusing His authority. *(From Lesson 6.5)*

## **Poetic Imagery and Hope (Lessons 6.6-6.7)**

### What does the shepherd imagery reveal about God's character?
**Answer:** The shepherd imagery reveals God as one who personally cares for, guides, protects, and provides for His people—He tends His flock, gathers lambs, and carries them close to His heart. *(From Lesson 6.6)*

### How does the shepherd imagery connect to Jesus in John 10?
**Answer:** Jesus declared "I am the Good Shepherd" who lays down His life for the sheep (John 10:11), fulfilling and surpassing all Old Testament shepherd imagery. *(From Lesson 6.6)*

### How do the prophets draw on Israel's past redemptive acts to inspire hope for the future?
**Answer:** Prophets referenced miraculous deliverances—Sarah's impossible birth of Isaac, Joseph's rise from slavery, the exodus, Red Sea crossing, and wilderness provision—to show that the same God who saved Israel in the past would save them again; their future hope was grounded in God's proven faithfulness. *(From Lesson 6.7)*

## **Prophecy and Fulfillment (Lessons 6.8-6.9)**

### What are the three time dimensions prophetic messages often addressed?
**Answer:** (1) **Present dimension**—addressed the immediate situation in the prophet's time; (2) **Near future**—events within generations including the Messiah's first coming; (3) **Distant future**—end times and Christ's second coming. *(From Lesson 6.8)*

### List specific details from Isaiah 53 about the coming servant.
**Answer:** Isaiah 53 describes the servant as despised and rejected, a man of suffering, pierced for our transgressions, crushed for our iniquities, by whose wounds we are healed; silent like a lamb to slaughter, assigned a grave with the wicked but buried with the rich. *(From Lesson 6.8)*

### How was Isaiah 53 fulfilled in Jesus?
**Answer:** Every detail was fulfilled in Jesus: rejected by His people, crucified between criminals, silent before Pilate, buried in Joseph of Arimathea's tomb, His death bringing healing and forgiveness. *(From Lesson 6.8)*

# **Unit 7: Exile and Return**

## **Daniel, Nebuchadnezzar, and The Fall of Babylon (Lesson 7.1-3)**

### What historical events brought Daniel to Babylon, and when did this happen?
**Answer:** King Nebuchadnezzar of Babylon besieged Jerusalem and took young men from the royal family and nobility to be trained for service in the Babylonian court; Daniel was taken during the first captivity around 606/605 BC during King Jehoiakim's reign. *(From Lesson 7.1)*

### What choice did Daniel and his friends face regarding the king's food?
**Answer:** The king assigned them daily portions of royal food and wine, which could have violated Jewish dietary laws OR could have been a form of "bribe" to buy their allegiance to Babylon. *(From Lesson 7.1)*

### Why was the king's food a problem for Daniel and his friends?
**Answer:** Eating it would defile them and compromise Daniel's commitment to God's—he resolved not to defile himself with the king's food and not to be bought with it. *(From Lesson 7.1)*

### How did God bless Daniel and his friends because they remained faithful to him?
**Answer:** After their ten-day vegetable test, they looked healthier than others; God gave them knowledge and understanding in all learning, and Daniel received special ability to interpret dreams and visions—they were found ten times better than all the king's magicians. *(From Lesson 7.1)*

### Describe Nebuchadnezzar's dream of the massive tree.
**Answer:** Nebuchadnezzar saw an enormous tree reaching to the sky, providing shelter and food for all creatures; a messenger from heaven ordered it cut down, leaving only a stump bound with iron and bronze. *(From Lesson 7.2)*

### What did the tree represent in Nebuchadnezzar's dream?
**Answer:** The tree represented Nebuchadnezzar himself—his greatness that had grown to reach the sky and his dominion that extended to the ends of the earth. *(From Lesson 7.2)*

### What warning did Daniel give to Nebuchadnezzar after interpreting his dream?
**Answer:** Daniel pleaded: "Renounce your sins by doing what is right, your wickedness by being kind to the oppressed"—if Nebuchadnezzar humbled himself before God, his prosperity might continue. *(From Lesson 7.2)*

### What happened to Nebuchadnezzar after Daniel's warning?
**Answer:** Twelve months later, while boasting about his achievements on the palace roof, Nebuchadnezzar was driven from people and lived like an animal with hair like eagles' feathers and nails like birds' claws. *(From Lesson 7.2)*

### How did Nebuchadnezzar respond after his humiliation?
**Answer:** He raised his eyes to heaven, acknowledged God's sovereignty, and was restored—he then praised and glorified God as King of heaven. *(From Lesson 7.2)*

### What supernatural event occurred during Belshazzar's feast, and how did he react?
**Answer:** A bodiless hand appeared and wrote on the palace wall; Belshazzar's face turned pale and his knees knocked together. *(From Lesson 7.3)*

### What did the words "Mene, Mene, Tekel, Parsin" mean?
**Answer:** MENE—God has numbered your reign and brought it to an end; TEKEL—you have been weighed and found wanting; PERES/PARSIN—your kingdom is divided and given to the Medes and Persians. *(From Lesson 7.3)*

### What did "Mene, Mene, Tekel, Parsin" signify for Babylon?
**Answer:** That very night Belshazzar was killed and Babylon fell to the Medes and Persians—God's judgment was swift and complete. *(From Lesson 7.3)*

## **Esther (Lessons 7.4-5)**

### What Jewish festival does the Book of Esther explain?
**Answer:** The Book of Esther explains the origin of the festival of Purim, which celebrates the deliverance of the Jewish people from Haman's plot to destroy them. *(From Lesson 7.4)*

### How did the festival of Purim get its name?
**Answer:** The name comes from "pur" (meaning "lot") because Haman cast lots to determine the date for the Jews' destruction, but God turned his evil plan against him. *(From Lesson 7.4)*

### What dangerous choice did Esther face when Mordecai asked her to intervene?
**Answer:** Esther had to decide whether to risk her life by approaching the king without being summoned (punishable by death) to reveal her Jewish identity and plead for her people; she hadn't been called for 30 days. *(From Lesson 7.4)*

### What was Esther's response to Mordecai's request?
**Answer:** She asked all Jews to fast for three days, then said "I will go to the king, even though it is against the law. And if I perish, I perish." *(From Lesson 7.4)*

## **The Exiles Return & Rebuilding the Walls (Lessons 7.6-9)**

### What did King Cyrus of Persia authorize?
**Answer:** Cyrus authorized the Jewish exiles to return to Jerusalem and rebuild the temple, and he returned the temple articles that Nebuchadnezzar had taken. *(From Lesson 7.6)*

### How does Ezra explain why Cyrus authorized the Jews to return?
**Answer:** Ezra explains: "The LORD moved the heart of Cyrus king of Persia"—God sovereignly worked through this pagan king to fulfill His purposes. *(From Lesson 7.6)*

### How does Jeremiah's prophecy connect to the return of the exiles under Cyrus?
**Answer:** Jeremiah had prophesied that the exile would last 70 years (Jeremiah 25:11-12); Cyrus's decree fulfilled this prophecy exactly, demonstrating God's faithfulness to His word spoken through the prophets. *(From Lesson 7.6)*

### What was the first thing the returning exiles built, and why was this significant?
**Answer:** The exiles first built the altar of God and began offering sacrifices morning and evening, even before the temple foundation was laid—this showed their priority to restore worship and their relationship with God above all else. *(From Lesson 7.6)*

### What forms of external opposition did Nehemiah face from Sanballat, Tobiah, and others?
**Answer:** Nehemiah faced: (1) ridicule and mockery ("what are those feeble Jews doing... even a fox would break down their wall"); (2) threats and plots to attack Jerusalem; (3) false accusations of rebellion; (4) attempts to lure him into a trap; and (5) hired prophets trying to discredit him. *(From Lesson 7.8)*

### What internal problems arose during Nehemiah's building project?
**Answer:** Wealthy Jews were exploiting poorer brothers—charging interest on loans and forcing them to mortgage fields, sell children into slavery, and lose property. *(From Lesson 7.8)*

### How did Nehemiah address the internal problems?
**Answer:** Nehemiah publicly rebuked the nobles and officials, commanded them to immediately return fields, vineyards, houses, and interest charged; he made them take an oath and they kept their promise. *(From Lesson 7.8)*

### How long did it take to rebuild the walls?
**Answer:** The walls were rebuilt in just 52 days. *(From Lesson 7.8)*

### What did even the enemies recognize about the wall-rebuilding accomplishment?
**Answer:** When the enemies heard, "all the surrounding nations were afraid and lost their self-confidence, because they realized that this work had been done with the help of our God"—even enemies were forced to acknowledge divine blessing. *(From Lesson 7.8)*
